# Medical debt & bankruptcy claims — user notes (for review)

## Claim 1: "66% of US bankruptcies are related to medical debt"

- Source: Himmelstein et al. 2019 AJPH study
- Their finding: 66.5% cite medical contributors (bills OR illness-related income loss OR >$1k medical debt)
- Key issue: "Related to" vs "caused by" - they measure correlation, not causation
- Critique from Dranove/Millenson: Only 17% when measuring true contribution
- Even in their own survey: Only 29% of respondents believed medical bills CAUSED their bankruptcy
- DOJ study: Medical debt was only 12-13% of total debts among filers who cited medical reasons

## Claim 2: "137 million Americans have medical debt"

- Source appears to be 2019 CNBC article citing unclear methodology
- More recent KFF 2022 survey: 41% of adults (100+ million) have healthcare debt
- CFPB 2024: 15 million have medical bills on credit reports ($49 billion)
- The "137 million" likely conflates "financial hardship" with actual "debt in collections"

## Claim 3: "Medical bills are a leading cause of bankruptcy"

- Contested - it's A major cause but causation is unclear
- Often conflated with income loss from illness
- Comparison: 78% cited income decline, 66.5% medical issues, 45% mortgage issues
- New England Journal of Medicine study: <4% of hospitalizations led to bankruptcy

## My assessment

1. 66% claim: EXAGGERATED - True for "contributors" but false for causation (likely 4-17%)
2. 137M claim: PARTIALLY TRUE but MISLEADING - Conflates hardship with collections debt
3. Leading cause claim: PARTIALLY TRUE - A major factor but causation unclear, often linked to income loss
